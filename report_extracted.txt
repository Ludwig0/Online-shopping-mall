Introduction
Project Background and Motivation
The publishing industry and retail landscape are undergoing a profound digital transformation. Current market trends clearly indicate a decisive shift toward online channels, particularly for books. Projections for 2026 estimate that 70–80% of all book sales will occur online, vastly outperforming the average of 20% for general retail. This dominance is further reinforced by consumer behavior: over 90% of shoppers read reviews before purchasing, and effective recommendation systems can boost sales by around 15%. These statistics collectively underscore a significant market opportunity and define the essential features of a modern online bookstore.
Despite this digital shift, independent bookstores and small publishers often struggle to compete, hindered by the high costs of physical operations and limited reach. Simultaneously, readers face challenges in discovering relevant titles within vast catalogs and frequently lack trusted, peer-sourced information to guide their purchases.
To address these interconnected challenges, this project aims to develop an intelligent and user-centric online bookstore platform. The system is designed to serve the core needs of its primary stakeholders:
For Readers: To provide a personalized and community-driven shopping experience that simplifies discovery and builds trust.
For Independent Booksellers & Small Publishers: To offer a cost-effective digital storefront with low operational overhead, enabling them to access a global customer base and manage their catalogs efficiently.
The platform will focus on the sale of books as its core product. The design will directly respond to the cited market data by an AI-powered recommendation engine, a robust review system, and strong security measures.




The proposed system is an online bookstore platform that supports end-to-end buying and selling activities for printed books. On the customer side, users can browse and search the catalogue, view detailed book information, add items to a shopping cart, place orders, and track order status. On the seller/administrator side, authorized staff can maintain the product catalogue, manage product information and images, process customer orders, update order statuses, and monitor inventory-related information. A key problem-domain consideration is that many books exist in multiple purchasable variants (e.g., paperback vs. hardcover, different editions, language versions, or bundle packages). To handle this realistically, the system models books as configurable products and manages availability at the variant level, so that customers can select the specific configuration they want and the platform can correctly reflect whether that configuration is currently available.
In addition to these general shopping-mall functions, the project highlights several features that directly respond to stakeholder needs. First, the platform includes a product recommendation capability to support readers in discovering relevant titles within a large catalogue; recommendations are presented in a practical form such as 'top rated' , 'similar books' , and 'you may also like' (based on your cart), helping users explore options beyond keyword search. Second, the system incorporates user-generated content by allowing readers to submit ratings and reviews, which are displayed with book listings to provide peer-sourced signals of quality and suitability—benefiting both readers (better purchase confidence) and sellers (greater credibility and engagement). Third, web application security is treated as a core requirement rather than an afterthought: the platform enforces proper authentication and role-based access control, protects common data-entry points through validation and CSRF defenses, and applies safe handling of uploaded content and sensitive operations. Together, these features aim to deliver a trustworthy online bookstore environment where readers can make informed choices, and independent booksellers can operate and manage their catalogue and orders with confidence.

Project Risk Management
Project risk management was conducted throughout the development of the online bookstore to reduce threats to the project goals (scope fulfilment, schedule, and achievable quality). Following the report template requirement, we identified three non-trivial risks with meaningful probability and/or impact, and prepared corresponding response plans. Our risk identification focused on the parts of the system that were most scope-critical and cross-cutting—namely the chosen functional blocks (A, B, D, S, T) and the non-functional security block (Z), whose issues could cascade across many modules.
Risk 1: Integration failure across cross-cutting features (configurable products, UGC, recommendations, and order workflow).
Description: Our selected scope includes tightly coupled modules: configurable products with SKU-level availability (Block D), user-generated content (Block T), recommendations (Block S), and multi-state order processing (Block B). These features share core data models (Product/SKU, Order/OrderItem, User interactions) and UI flows (product detail → cart → checkout → order history). The risk is that independently developed parts may conflict at integration time (e.g., SKU option selection not consistently reflected in cart/order records, recommendation widgets assuming missing fields, review permissions mismatching authentication state).
Probability of occurrence: Medium (multiple parallel implementations with shared data contracts).
Impact: High. Integration defects can block end-to-end user flows (browse/search → add to cart → checkout → track order), causing missed scope items or unstable demonstrations, and requiring costly rework late in the schedule.
Response: We applied an “integration-first” strategy: (i) define and freeze core data contracts early (SKU representation, order line items, review ownership), (ii) run weekly integrated builds with a minimal but complete shopping flow, and (iii) prioritize automated sanity checks on the critical paths (add configurable SKU to cart, checkout creates correct order items, order status transitions remain valid). We also used a staged milestone plan where core shopping flow integration was validated before adding advanced features.
Risk 2: Data quality and sparsity risk for recommendations and user-generated content.
Description: Block S (recommendations) and Block T (UGC) depend on reliable user behaviour signals and/or rating data. In a student project setting, the system lacks natural traffic, so interaction logs and review coverage can be sparse or biased. In addition, if imported catalog data is inconsistent (missing categories/authors, inconsistent identifiers), both “related books” logic and recommendation quality degrade, and UI components can fail on edge cases. The system specification explicitly expects students to perform additional analysis for further requirements, which increases the chance that data issues surface late if not planned early.
Probability of occurrence: Medium to High (limited real users; manually curated datasets).
Impact: Medium to High. Poor data can (i) reduce perceived usefulness of recommendations, (ii) lead to empty/incorrect widgets (hurting UX), and (iii) increase debugging cost due to non-deterministic “works on my data” behaviour—risking schedule and demonstration readiness.
Response: We mitigated this risk through controlled dataset engineering and fallbacks: (i) prepare a seed dataset with consistent categories/authors and variant/SKU coverage; (ii) seed a small but representative set of ratings/reviews to ensure the UGC pipeline and permissions can be demonstrated; (iii) implement graceful-degradation logic for recommendation display (e.g., fall back to “related by category/author” when collaborative signals are insufficient); and (iv) add validation scripts/checks to catch missing key fields before import. This ensures the recommendation and UGC features remain functional and demonstrable under sparse-data conditions.
Risk 3: Security vulnerabilities and misconfiguration affecting authentication, authorization, and input handling.
Description: Block Z requires robust web application security, and security issues are cross-cutting: authentication/authorization, form submissions, file uploads (e.g., product images), and user-generated content (reviews). Misconfigurations or insecure defaults can lead to common web risks (e.g., CSRF exposure on state-changing requests, insufficient access control for admin functions, unsafe handling of user inputs in reviews). Because security hardening often spans many endpoints and templates, late discovery can force broad refactoring and delay completion.
Probability of occurrence: Medium (multiple forms/pages; complex permission boundaries).
Impact: High. A security flaw can invalidate the credibility of the system, risk data leakage or account misuse, and reduce the attainable quality grade. It can also break key functions during demonstration if emergency fixes introduce regressions.
Response: We adopted a “secure-by-design” approach: (i) enforce role-based access control for administrative operations from the beginning, (ii) ensure all state-changing forms follow CSRF-safe patterns and server-side validation, (iii) restrict uploaded content types and storage paths for product images, and (iv) run a security checklist as part of integration testing (covering authentication boundaries, permission checks, and injection/XSS/CSRF-related cases). Security work was scheduled alongside feature development, not postponed to the end, to avoid large late-stage rewrites.
Overall, these three risks were tracked as living items during weekly planning and integration, with responses embedded into our milestones and testing strategy to protect the delivery of the selected blocks and the final demonstration quality.

Retrospective on Team Dynamic
Our team formed around a shared goal of delivering a complete and believable online bookstore. In practice, the project demanded frequent coordination because many modules were cross-cutting: configurable variants affect product pages, cart/checkout, and order records; reviews influence product presentation and trust; recommendations must integrate with catalogue and user interactions. This coupling made communication and integration discipline more important than isolated coding speed.
Challenges encountered and impact.
First, we faced scheduling friction due to different weekly timetables and uneven available time near deadlines; this sometimes delayed reviews of pull requests and reduced the window for integration testing. Second, the team had different strengths (some members were more backend-oriented while others were stronger in UI, data processing, or security). This initially caused mismatched expectations about what “done” meant for a feature (e.g., implemented but not yet testable through a complete user flow). Third, integration issues emerged when parallel work touched shared models and templates, creating merge conflicts and occasional regressions that increased debugging effort near milestone dates.
How we addressed the issues.
We adopted a coordination routine with a predictable cadence: short weekly planning, clear ownership per block, and frequent “integration checkpoints” to validate the end-to-end purchase flow before adding advanced features. When conflicts occurred, we prioritized stabilizing shared data contracts (e.g., SKU/variant representation, order item fields, review ownership rules) and used a lightweight definition of done: a feature is considered complete only if it is reachable through the UI, behaves correctly for common and edge cases, and does not break the core shopping path. We also improved collaboration by enforcing consistent Git practices (feature branches and small merges), using code review to catch permission/security issues early, and maintaining a running report draft so that documentation was not postponed to the end.
What we would improve next time.
If we run a similar project again, we would formalize the integration plan even earlier by freezing shared interfaces sooner and writing a small set of automated integration tests for the critical flows from Week 3–4 onward. We would also allocate explicit “buffer days” before each milestone for cross-testing on multiple machines and for security checks, rather than assuming testing can be fully absorbed into the final week. Finally, we would keep a more structured decision log (short meeting notes with decisions and rationale) so that later report writing and design justification becomes faster and more consistent.

Methods for Gathering Information and Establishing Specification
1. Research Approach and Core Methodology
Our primary method was competitive feature analysis. We systematically deconstructed the user experience of leading platforms to understand how they solve common problems. This was supplemented by domain research into book purchasing behaviors, which highlighted the paramount importance of discovery aids and social proof in this market.
2. Reference Systems and Feature Modeling
We strategically referenced three categories of systems:
Amazon[1]: Served as our model for core e-commerce infrastructure. We adopted its proven patterns for:
Site-wide navigation: Including header with branding, shopping cart, user account, and address management.
Product detail page layout: A clear presentation of core attributes (title, author, price, description).
Configurable product interface: The model for selling different formats (paperback, hardcover, audiobook) of the same title, which directly informed our implementation of Block D.
Douban Reading[2]: Served as our model for building trust in book purchases. We integrated its core value proposition:
Rating and review system: A structured space for user-generated scores and detailed textual reviews, forming the basis of our Block T (User-Generated Content). Unlike Douban, we embedded this system directly into the purchasing flow rather than as a separate community.
Dangdang[3]: Served as a reference for domain-specific sales tactics.
"You May Also Like" recommendations: This feature inspired our approach to Block S (Product Recommendations), where we employ AI to analyze user behavior and suggest relevant books.
3. Adaptations for Our Product (Books) and Project Scope
We did not copy features verbatim but adapted them for our context:
Simplification: We focused Douban's broad social features down to the essential rating and review system, aligning with our core goal of facilitating sales, not building a social network.
Domain Specialization: We tailored Amazon's generic "configurable product" model specifically for book formats and editions (e.g., ebook vs. paperback), making it more intuitive for book buyers.
Integration: A key insight was to seamlessly integrate these borrowed features. Reviews (from Douban) and format choices (from Amazon) are presented on the same product page, creating a unified decision-making experience for the reader.
4. Analysis Scheme and Fulfilled Requirements
Our analysis proceeded in phases: 1) Benchmarking and feature extraction, 2) Mapping features to the System Specification blocks, 3) Prioritizing based on project scope and the book domain, 4) Finalizing the feature set.
Through this process, we identified more potential requirements than could be implemented (e.g., a social "bookshelf" feature, advanced book trading). The requirements we have successfully fulfilled in the developed system are Blocks A, B, D, S, T, and Z. A complete account of their implementation status and where to find more details in this report is provided in the consolidated table in Section 3.3.

Technical Choices & Rationale
Why Django?
Django was chosen over Flask or FastAPI primarily due to the team's existing familiarity, which ensures efficient development within the course timeline. Beyond familiarity, Django's batteries-included nature provides built-in authentication, admin panel, and ORM—eliminating the need to integrate multiple third-party packages[1][2]. This enables rapid prototyping while maintaining clean code architecture.
Why No JavaScript Framework?
We opted not to use React, Vue, or Bootstrap to keep the stack simple and Python-focused. Django Templates handle all UI requirements adequately, and avoiding client-side complexity reduces development time and potential bugs. The project's value lies in the backend AI recommendation system, not frontend interactivity.
Why SQLite?
SQLite was chosen for development because it requires zero configuration and is highly portable—the entire database is a single file that team members can share instantly[3]. With only 250 books and 3,500 reviews, SQLite handles our dataset without performance issues, and Django's ORM allows seamless migration to PostgreSQL later if needed.
Why Word2Vec for Recommendations?
We chose Word2Vec over traditional collaborative filtering due to limited user interaction data (only 5 reviews per book on average). Word2Vec is a shallow neural network invented at Google that learns semantic relationships between words from product descriptions, titles, authors, and categories[4]. Each product is represented as a vector by averaging the embeddings of its associated words. Cosine similarity between these vectors enables content-based recommendations without requiring user behavior data—ideal for cold-start scenarios.
The recommendation system includes:
Similar Products: Word2Vec-based semantic similarity
Top Rated: Aggregated review scores and counts
Cart Recommendations: Category-based matching with randomization
This hybrid approach balances AI depth with practical constraints, delivering relevant recommendations while keeping the system lightweight and maintainable.

4.3.1 State Diagram & explain
pending: The initial state upon order creation.The system triggers Inventory Reservation to ensure the book is not oversold.
Hold: A critical buffer state for the bookstore. If a book is found to be damaged or out of stock during picking, the order is held here.  This gives customer service time to contact the reader and resolve the issue (e.g.,offering a discount or a replacement) before the book is actually shipped.
Shipped: The final successful outcome. Once the book is shipped, the data is locked to prevent tampering in the future, ensuring that the accounts are completely consistent with the logistics.
Cancelled:The final unsuccessful outcome. Upon cancellation, the system automatically executes a Stock Release, returning the book to the virtual shelf for other readers.


4.3.2 Sequence of Operations
1.   The Primary Path (Success): An order begins as Pending (stock reserved). If the book is available and intact, it is Shipped (final success), and the data is locked to ensure consistency.
2.  Auxiliary Paths (Exceptions): If a Condition Issue (e.g., damage) is found, the order enters Hold for review.  From there, it is either Shipped (resolved) or Cancelled (rejected).  A Reader Cancel also leads to Cancelled (final failure), which triggers a Stock Release for other users.

To enhance purchase confidence and provide peer-based trust signals, the system implements a structured user-generated content (UGC) module that allows authenticated customers to submit ratings and textual reviews for products they have purchased.
4.4.1Data Model Design
Customer reviews are represented by the CustomerReview entity:
class CustomerReview(models.Model):
    product = models.ForeignKey(Product, on_delete=models.CASCADE, related_name='customer_reviews')
    user = models.ForeignKey(settings.AUTH_USER_MODEL, on_delete=models.CASCADE, related_name='customer_reviews')
    rating = models.PositiveSmallIntegerField()  # 1–5
    review_text = models.TextField()

    class Meta:
        unique_together = ('product', 'user')
Each review is linked to a specific Product and User, forming a many-to-one relationship. The unique_together = ('product', 'user') constraint enforces a business rule at the database level: one user can submit only one review per product, preventing duplication even under concurrent requests. Ratings are stored as bounded integers (1–5), ensuring structured evaluative data.
4.4.2Access Control and Purchase Verification
To maintain review credibility, the system restricts review submission to customers who have actually purchased the product. This is enforced through a purchase validation function:
def user_has_purchased_product(user, product):
    return PurchaseOrder.objects.filter(
        customer=user,
        items__product=product
    ).exclude(status=PurchaseOrder.STATUS_CANCELLED).exists()
The submission endpoint is protected with @login_required, and purchase verification is performed before accepting the form:
@login_required
def submit_product_review(request, product_id):
    if not user_has_purchased_product(request.user, product):
        messages.error(request, "Only customers who purchased this product can submit a review.")
        return redirect('product_detail', slug=product.slug)
This layered control mechanism ensures that reviews originate from verified buyers, strengthening the trustworthiness of displayed feedback.
4.4.3Form Handling and Update Logic
The system allows users to both submit and update their review. If a review already exists, it is passed as the instance to the form:
existing_review = CustomerReview.objects.filter(
    product=product,
    user=request.user
).first()
form = CustomerReviewForm(request.POST, instance=existing_review)
This design avoids duplicate records and enables seamless review editing while preserving the one-review-per-user constraint.

On the product detail page, both imported external reviews and internal customer reviews are rendered:
customer_reviews = product.customer_reviews.select_related('user').all()

Top Rated Products (Statistical)
The homepage displays books with the highest average ratings, helping first-time visitors quickly identify popular titles. This is implemented using Django's aggregation framework:
def get_top_rated_products(limit=10):    """Return top-rated products based on average rating and review count"""    return Product.objects.annotate(        avg_rating=Avg('customer_reviews__rating'),        review_count=Count('customer_reviews')    ).filter(        review_count__gte=1  # At least one review    ).order_by('-avg_rating', '-review_count')[:limit]
Design Rationale:
Simple and interpretable
No cold-start problem (works for all products with reviews)
Real-time query, always up-to-date with latest ratings
Similar Products (Word2Vec-based)
This is the core AI-powered feature. The system recommends books that are semantically similar to the one currently being viewed, based on product textual data.
Key Implementation Challenge: Performance
The main challenge was balancing recommendation quality with response time. Computing cosine similarity between a product and all others in real-time would require O(n²) operations per request, which doesn't scale as the catalog grows.
Solution: Precomputation Strategy
Instead of online computation, we precompute the full similarity matrix offline:
# Offline precomputation (runs once)norms = np.linalg.norm(product_vectors, axis=1, keepdims=True)norms[norms == 0] = 1normalized_vectors = product_vectors / normssimilarity_matrix=np.dot(normalized_vectors, normalized_vectors.T)
This reduces online queries to O(1) matrix lookup:
# Online query (O(1))similar_indices = np.argsort(similarity_matrix[product_idx])[-6:-1][::-1]
Handling Cold-Start Products
Another challenge was recommending newly added products with no user interaction data. Word2Vec solves this naturally since recommendations are based on product content, not user behavior. New products are immediately recommendable once their text is processed. This is a key advantage over collaborative filtering, which cannot recommend items without prior user interactions.
Cart-based Recommendations (Heuristic)
A simpler but effective approach for the shopping cart page:
def get_cart_recommendations(cart, limit=5):    categories = cart.items.values_list(    'variant__product__category', flat=True).distinct()    cart_product_ids = cart.items.values_list(    'variant__product_id', flat=True)        return Product.objects.filter(        category__in=categories,        is_active=True    ).exclude(        id__in=cart_product_ids    ).order_by('?')[:limit]  # Random order for variety
Interesting Design Choice: The order_by('?') clause provides randomization, ensuring users see different recommendations across sessions. This prevents the "same recommendations every time" fatigue that can occur with static recommendation lists.
